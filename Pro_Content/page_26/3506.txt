<div class="plm"><table align="center"><tr><td><b>Time Limit:</b> 1000MS</td><td width="10px"></td><td><b>Memory Limit:</b> 65536K</td></tr><tr><td><b>Total Submissions:</b> 442</td><td width="10px"></td><td><b>Accepted:</b> 145</td></table></div><p class="pst">Description</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><p>Alice would like to visit Bob. However, they live in a hilly landscape, and Alice doesn&rsquo;t like to walk in hills. She has a map of the area, showing the height curves. You have to calculate the total altitude climbed, and the total altitude descended, for the route which minimizes these numbers. It does not matter how far she has to walk to achieve this.</p><p>Since you don&rsquo;t know what the landscape looks like in between the height curves, you cannot know exactly how much climb and descent she will actually get in practice, but you should calculate the minimum possible under optimal conditions based on what you can deduce from the map.</p><p>The map is represented as an <i>xy</i> grid. Alice lives in (0, 0), and Bob lives in (<nobr>100 000</nobr>, 0). The height curves are represented as polygons, where a polygon cannot intersect itself or another polygon. Furthermore, neither Alice nor Bob lives exactly on a height curve.</p><div align="center"><img src="images/3506_1.png"></div><p align="center">Second test case from sample input (compressed).</p></div><p class="pst">Input</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><p>On the first line one positive number: the number of testcases, at most 100. After that per testcase:</p><ul><li><p>One line with 0 &le; <i>N</i> &le; <nobr>2 500</nobr>, the number of height curves.</p></li><li><p>One line for each height curve, with 1 &le; <i>H<sub>i</sub></i> &le; <nobr>1 000</nobr> being the height of the curve, 3 &le; <i>P<sub>i</sub></i> &le; <nobr>2 000</nobr> the number of vertices in the polygon, and the vertices <i>x</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>y</i><sub>1</sub>, &hellip;, <i>x<sub>P<sub>i</sub></sub></i>, <i>y<sub>P<sub>i</sub></sub></i> having integral values <nobr>&minus;300 000</nobr> &le; <i>x<sub>i</sub></i>, <i>y<sub>i</sub></i> &le; <nobr>300 000</nobr>.</p></li></ul><p>There will be no more than <nobr>200 000</nobr> polygon vertices in total in all test cases.</p></div><p class="pst">Output</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><p>Per testcase:</p><ul><li>One line with two numbers: the total altitude climbed and the total altitude descended.</li></ul></div><p class="pst">Sample Input</p><pre class="sio">2
