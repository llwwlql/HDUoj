<div class="plm"><table align="center"><tr><td><b>Time Limit:</b> 1000MS</td><td width="10px"></td><td><b>Memory Limit:</b> 65536K</td></tr><tr><td><b>Total Submissions:</b> 523</td><td width="10px"></td><td><b>Accepted:</b> 321</td></table></div><p class="pst">Description</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><span lang="en-us"><blockquote><p style="text-indent: 4ex; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0">&hellip; Los babilonios se entregaron al juego. El que no adquir&iacute;a suertes era considerado un pusil&aacute;nime, un apocado. Con el tiempo, ese desd&eacute;n justificado se duplic&oacute;. Era despreciado el que no jugaba, pero tambi&eacute;n era despreciados los perdedores que abonaban la multa &hellip;</p><p style="text-indent: 4ex; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0" align="right">Jorge Luis Borges, La loter&iacute;a de Babilonia, Ficciones</p></blockquote><p>People of Babylon were devoted to chance games and one of the most popular was a special kind of roulette. Recently, some old Babylonian tablets were found. They described details of the roulette game.</p><p>In modern terms, the rules of the game were as follows:</p><ul><li>Roulette&rsquo;s compartments had only six labels: <code>-1, -2, -3, 1, 2, 3</code>.</li><li>The game was played by turns, during a day. Turns were numerated <code>0, 1, 2, ...</code></li><li>Players could win or lose a multiple of the <i>bet</i>, a quantity of money that was constant along the day.</li><li>At turn <i>t</i> there was an amount of money <i>P<sub>t</sub></i>, called the <i>pot</i>.</li><li>At the start, there was an initial amount of money <i>P</i><sub>0</sub> in the pot.</li><li><i>P</i><sub>0</sub> and the bet were positive numbers arbitrarily defined by the King.</li><li>In a turn, a player turned the roulette. A player could not play more than once in a day. Depending on the compartment where the ball came to rest, the player won (or lose, if the value was negative) an mount <i>w<sub>t</sub></i> = <i>L</i> * <i>bet</i> of money, where <i>L</i> corresponded to the compartment&rsquo;s label.</li><li>The won money was taken from the pot (or put in it if the player lose), i.e. the value of the pot in a given turn was determined by <i>P<sub>t</sub></i><sub>+1</sub> = <i>P<sub>t</sub></i> + <i>w<sub>t</sub></i>.</li>  <li>If as a result of the last rule <i>P<sub>t</sub></i><sub>+1</sub> was a negative number the winner won only the maximum multiple of the bet that he could win without making a negative pot.</li><li>If at some turn the pot was less than the bet, the game was ended for that day. If that was not the case the game continued till sunset.</li></ul><p>Beside the tablets that explained the rules some other tablets were found. These had lines with three numbers. Archeologists conjecture that each of these lines were part of a kind of accountability system for the game, where numbers represented, for a given day, the value of the pot at the beginning, the bet and the value of the pot at the end.</p><p>For example, a line with the number</p><p><code>10000 1500 11500</code></p><p>could mean that there was only one turn where the player won with label <code>1</code>. Another possibility is that there were three turns with results <code>2</code>, <code>1</code> and <code>-2</code>.</p><p>On the other hand, there were found other tablets with triplets of numbers that seem like the above described that, however, cannot represent results of a game day. There is no hypothesis of what they are.</p><p>Archeologists want to validate their hypothesis analyzing batches of tablets with triplets. They want to estimate the number of people that played in a day. To begin, they want to establish, for each triplet of numbers in a tablet that could represent a result of a game day, the minimal number of players that played that day. In the above example the answer to this question is <code>1</code>. Tablets that cannot represent results should be identified. You are hired to help with this task.</p></span></div><p class="pst">Input</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><span lang="en-us"<p>The input file contains several test cases, each one of them in a separate line. Each test case is a triplet of non negative integers, indicating the initial pot, the bet and the final pot for a day. Each of the input numbers is less than 10<sup>8</sup>. The initial pot and the bet are greater than 0.</p><p>A line with a triplet of <code>0</code>&rsquo;s denotes the end of the input.</p></div><p class="pst">Output</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><p>Output texts for each input case are presented in the same order that input is read. For each test case the answer must be a printed line.</p><p>If the test case cannot represent the result of a game day, the output line has the words <code>No accounting tablet</code>. In other case, the printed answer is one positive integer number telling the minimal number of players that could turn the roulette for the day corresponding to the annotations.</p></div><p class="pst">Sample Input</p><pre class="sio">10000 1000 22000
24 13 2
5100 700 200
54 16 158
360 6 72
25 10 5
0 0 0</pre><p class="pst">Sample Output</p><pre class="sio">4
No accounting tablet
3
No accounting tablet
16
1</pre><p class="pst">Hint</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><span lang="en-us"><p>English translation of the Spanish preface:</p><blockquote><p style="text-indent: 4ex; margin-bottom: 0">&hellip; Babylonians flocked to buy tickets. The man who bought none was considered a pusillanimous wretch, a man with no spirit of adventure. In time, this justified contempt found a second target: not just the man who didn&rsquo;t play, but also the man who lost and paid the fine. &hellip;</p><p style="text-indent: 4ex; margin-top: 0" align="right">Jorge Luis Borges, The Lottery in Babylon</p></blockquote><p>(cited from <a href="http://www.hfac.uh.edu/mcl/faculty/armstrong/cityofdreams/texts/babylon.html">here</a>)</p></span></div><p class="pst">Source</p><div class="ptx" lang="en-US"><a href="searchproblem?field=source&key=Colombia+2006">Colombia 2006</a></div></td></tr></table>
